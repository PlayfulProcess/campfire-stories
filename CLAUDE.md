# CLAUDE.md — The Campfire Stories

## Privacy

The author publishes under the name **PlayfulProcess**. Never use any real/personal names anywhere in this repository.

## What This Is

Five-minute bedtime stories for ages 3–7, built from cross-cultural story arcs. Each "book" contains two paired stories — like Daniel Tiger episodes — that explore the same theme from two different traditions.

## Audience

Parents who want better bedtime stories than what the algorithm serves. Also educators, librarians, and anyone who reads aloud to children.

## Voice

Warm, rhythmic, meant to be read aloud. Simple vocabulary but not condescending. Think Fred Rogers meets Hayao Miyazaki.

**Rules:**
- Short sentences. Concrete images. No jargon.
- Every sentence must scan when spoken aloud. If it stumbles, rewrite it.
- Refrains should be 4–8 syllables, chantable by a 4-year-old.
- Darkness is allowed — darkness with a container. The scary part always has an embrace around it.
- Never use: "delve," "landscape," "tapestry," "multifaceted," "it's important to note."
- Never start paragraphs with "In the..." or "When it comes to..."

## Methodology: The Three-Filter Test

Every story must pass all three filters:

1. **Useful** — Builds a specific developmental skill (emotional regulation, courage, empathy, problem-solving, etc.)
2. **Fits the data** — Developmentally appropriate for ages 3–7. Honors the source tradition without extracting from it. Aligns with what we know about how children process narrative.
3. **Compassionate** — Serves the child's growth. The darkness has a container. No child is shamed, dismissed, or left alone in the scary part.

## Story Structure

Each story follows a consistent arc:
1. **The world** — Establish the setting and character in 2–3 sentences
2. **The trouble** — Something goes wrong or something scary appears
3. **The refrain** — A repeatable phrase the child can join in on (appears 3 times)
4. **The turning point** — The character finds courage, cleverness, or help
5. **The resolution** — Safety returns, but the character has grown
6. **The warmth** — A closing image of connection, rest, or home

## Source Traditions

Each story draws from a specific cultural storytelling tradition. At the end of every story, include a **Source Tradition Note** — 2–3 sentences explaining where the story arc comes from and honoring the tradition. Never claim the story IS a traditional tale — it is inspired by the tradition's patterns and values.

Traditions drawn from so far:
- **Akan/Ashanti** — Anansi spider-trickster stories (West Africa → Caribbean)
- **Jātaka** — Buddhist birth stories (India → Southeast Asia)
- **Aboriginal Australian** — Dreamtime creation narratives
- **Haudenosaunee** — Skywoman and gratitude traditions (Turtle Island)
- **Grimm/European** — Folk tale patterns (pre-sanitization spirit)
- **1001 Nights** — Scheherazade's nested stories (Arabic/Persian)
- **Japanese** — Yomikikase read-aloud tradition, mukashi-banashi folk tales

## Book Plan

- **Book 1: When Things Feel Scary** — Stories about courage and fear (Anansi + Jātaka)
- **Book 2: When Someone Needs Help** — Stories about compassion and sacrifice (Jātaka + Haudenosaunee)
- **Book 3: When the World Feels Too Big** — Stories about finding your place (Dreamtime + Japanese)
- **Book 4: When Things Aren't Fair** — Stories about cleverness and justice (Anansi + 1001 Nights)
- **Book 5: When You Feel Different** — Stories about belonging and gifts (Grimm + Haudenosaunee)

## Important Constraints

- Respect CARE Principles for Indigenous Data (Collective Benefit, Authority, Responsibility, Ethics)
- Never claim these ARE traditional stories. They are inspired by traditional patterns.
- Never fabricate quotes or attribute words to real cultural figures.
- Target ~800–1,000 words per story (roughly 5 minutes read aloud).
- Include read-aloud tips (where to pause, where to whisper, where to let the child join in).
