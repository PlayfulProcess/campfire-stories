# The Deer Who Stood Still

*Book 1, Story B — When Things Feel Scary*

---

In the tall grass at the edge of the river, there lived a young deer named Kavi.

Kavi had brown eyes, a white tail, and legs so fast that when he ran, even the wind had to sit down and catch its breath. He could leap over logs. He could dodge between trees. He could sprint from the river to the mango grove in the time it took his mother to blink twice.

But Kavi had a problem. Whenever he heard a loud sound — a branch cracking, a bird screaming, thunder grumbling in its sleep — he ran.

He didn't think. He didn't look. He just ran.

And sometimes, running was exactly the wrong thing to do.

---

One morning, Kavi's mother took him to the river to drink.

"Listen," she said. "The river has something to teach you."

Kavi listened. The river gurgled and splashed and tumbled over rocks.

"What is it saying?" he asked.

His mother dipped her nose in the cool water. "It's saying: *not every sound is a danger, and not every danger needs running.*"

Kavi frowned. "But my legs are made for running."

"Your legs are made for choosing," his mother said. "Running is one choice. Standing still is another. Knowing which one to pick — that's what keeps you safe."

---

That afternoon, a branch cracked in the forest behind them.

Kavi's legs twitched. His heart hammered. Every part of him screamed: *run run run run run.*

But his mother stood still. Her ears turned. Her nose lifted. Her dark eyes searched the shadows between the trees.

*"Ears up. Nose out. Breathe before you run."*

She said it calmly, like it was a song she'd been singing her whole life.

Kavi tried. He locked his legs. He pointed his ears forward. He breathed — one long, shaky breath.

The shadows shifted. A porcupine waddled out from behind a log, grumbling to itself about the quality of bark these days.

Not a tiger. Not a hunter. A porcupine.

"If you'd run," his mother said, "you would have run straight toward the cliff path. That's more dangerous than any porcupine."

Kavi's heart was still pounding. But his legs had held.

---

The next day, a bird screamed overhead — sharp and sudden, like a thorn made of sound.

Kavi's muscles fired. His back legs coiled.

*"Ears up. Nose out. Breathe before you run."*

He said it himself this time. Out loud. His voice wobbled, but he said it.

He looked up. A peacock was fighting with a crow over a fig. Feathers everywhere. A lot of noise. No danger at all.

Kavi let his breath out slowly. His legs softened. The world came back into focus — the grass, the river, the warm smell of mud and morning.

"Good," said his mother from across the clearing. "You stood."

---

On the third day, the real sound came.

Low. Steady. A growl that lived in the ground before it reached the air. The kind of sound that makes the birds go silent and the insects hold their breath.

Kavi's mother was already on her feet.

*"Ears up. Nose out. Breathe before you run."*

He said it. He breathed. He listened.

And this time, the sound was real. A shape moved in the tall grass — orange and black, low and patient. A tiger.

"Now," his mother said. One word. Quiet as a leaf falling.

And they ran.

Not blindly. Not in panic. They ran together, side by side, toward the river crossing where the water was shallow and the rocks were flat and a deer could keep its footing but a tiger could not. They ran the way the river runs — knowing where it's going, choosing its path, fast and sure and alive.

The tiger stopped at the water's edge. It watched them cross. It turned and disappeared back into the tall grass, looking for something slower and less certain.

---

That night, Kavi lay in the grass beside his mother. The stars were out. The river whispered its same old song.

"Mama," he said. "How do you know when to stand and when to run?"

She rested her chin on his back. "You stand when you need to see clearly. You run when you've seen enough. The standing comes first. Always."

Kavi closed his eyes. His legs, those wonderful fast legs, were still. His heart was slow. The grass was warm beneath him and the stars were steady above him and his mother's breath moved against his fur like a tide coming in.

He was not afraid.

Or — he was afraid. But he knew what to do with it now. And that was almost the same thing.

---

*Snuggle close. Close your eyes. Tomorrow there might be loud sounds and scary shadows. But you know the secret now: ears up, nose out, breathe before you run.*

### Read-Aloud Tips

- **The refrain** ("Ears up. Nose out. Breathe before you run.") — do the actions with your child. Point your ears up (cup your hands behind your ears). Sniff the air dramatically. Take one big breath together. By the third time, they'll lead it.
- **The porcupine** — make it grumpy and funny. Porcupines are comedy gold for this age group.
- **The tiger moment** — drop your voice low. Go slow. The silence before "Now" is the scariest and most important part. Then speed up — the running should feel fast in your voice.
- **The ending** — slow way down. Match your breathing to the rhythm. Your child's body will sync with yours. This is co-regulation in action.

### Three-Filter Analysis

| Filter | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Useful** | Builds emotional regulation — specifically the pause between stimulus and response. Models the difference between reactive fear (run from everything) and responsive fear (assess, then act). Also models that sometimes running IS the right choice — the skill is discernment, not suppression. |
| **Fits the data** | Aligns with polyvagal theory — the story literally teaches a child to move from sympathetic activation (fight/flight) to ventral vagal assessment (ears up, nose out, breathe) before deciding on action. The three-day structure provides graduated exposure. Developmentally appropriate for ages 3–7. |
| **Compassionate** | Kavi is never shamed for wanting to run. His mother doesn't say running is wrong — she says it's one choice among several. The refrain gives the child a tool they can actually use (ears up, nose out, breathe) rather than an abstract instruction ("calm down"). The darkness (the tiger) is real, and the response to real danger is honored — they DO run, when running is right. |

### Source Tradition Note

This story is inspired by the **Jātaka tradition** — 547 stories of the Buddha's previous births in human and animal form, used for ethical education across South and Southeast Asia for over 2,500 years. The Nigrodhamiga Jātaka (#12), in which a deer king offers himself in place of a pregnant doe, is among the most beloved. Jātaka tales frequently use animal characters to teach that compassion, discernment, and self-sacrifice are higher than mere survival. This is not a retelling of any specific Jātaka, but a new story written in the tradition's spirit of inter-species moral education and graduated ethical development. The Jātaka collection is preserved in the Pali Canon and depicted in temple art across Thailand, Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Cambodia, and Indonesia.
