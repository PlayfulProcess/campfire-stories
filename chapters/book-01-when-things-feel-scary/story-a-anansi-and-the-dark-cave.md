# Anansi and the Dark Cave

*Book 1, Story A — When Things Feel Scary*

---

Anansi the Spider had eight legs, eight shoes, and one very big problem.

There was a cave at the edge of the forest. A dark cave. A deep cave. A cave where the shadows lived so close together you couldn't tell where one ended and the next began.

And inside that cave — everybody said so — there was a drum.

Not just any drum. The Drum of Stories. Whoever played it could hear every story in the world, all at once, like rain on a wide green leaf. Anansi wanted that drum more than he had ever wanted anything. More than mangoes. More than honey. More than the cool side of a rock on a hot afternoon.

But the cave was dark.

And Anansi — clever, quick, brave-when-he-felt-like-it Anansi — was afraid of the dark.

---

He went to Turtle first.

"Turtle," he said, "will you come into the dark cave with me?"

Turtle pulled her head inside her shell. "No thank you," she said from inside. "I have my own dark in here. That's enough for me."

He went to Parrot.

"Parrot," he said, "will you come into the dark cave with me?"

Parrot ruffled her feathers. "I could," she said. "But I won't. My colors don't work in the dark, and what's a parrot without colors?"

He went to Elephant.

"Elephant," he said, "will you come into the dark cave with me? You're the biggest. You're not afraid of anything."

Elephant swayed from one enormous foot to the other. "I'm afraid of plenty," she said softly. "Mice. Bees. The sound my own stomach makes at midnight. I won't go in that cave, Anansi. But I'll wait for you right here, at the mouth. I'll be here when you come back out."

Anansi looked at the cave. He looked at Elephant. He looked at his eight shoes, every one of them trembling.

"What if I don't come back out?" he whispered.

Elephant curled her trunk around him, gentle and warm. "Then I'll come in and get you," she said.

---

Anansi walked to the mouth of the cave. The darkness breathed out, cool and damp, like the forest after rain.

He put one shoe in. Then two. Then three and four.

*"Small legs, strong heart, one step more in the dark."*

He said it out loud. Not because it was magic. Because his voice was the only thing he could bring into the dark that the dark couldn't swallow.

Five shoes in. Six. The light from outside shrank to a sliver behind him, thin as a thread, thin as a wish.

*"Small legs, strong heart, one step more in the dark."*

Something dripped. Something rustled. Anansi's eight knees knocked together — *tak-tak-tak-tak-tak-tak-tak-tak* — like a tiny, terrified drum.

And then he heard it.

A real drum. Far ahead, deep inside, beating slow and steady. *Boom. Boom. Boom.* Like a heart. Like the world's own heart, buried under stone and shadow and all the things that everyone was afraid of.

He followed the sound.

*"Small legs, strong heart, one step more in the dark."*

---

The cave opened into a wide, round room, and the drum sat in the center on a flat stone. It was smaller than Anansi expected. Plain wood. Simple hide. Nothing special to look at.

But when he reached out one trembling leg and tapped it — just once, just barely —

The whole cave filled with sound.

Not just drumming. Stories. He could hear them. A grandmother singing to a baby by a river. Two brothers lost in the snow, finding each other by the sound of their breathing. A girl planting seeds in a cracked, dry field, believing the rain would come.

He heard a story about a spider who was afraid of the dark.

He heard how that story ended: the spider went in anyway.

---

Anansi carried the drum out of the cave. It was light — lighter than he expected, like it had been waiting to be carried, like it wanted to be found.

Elephant was waiting, just like she'd promised, right at the mouth.

"You went in," she said.

"I did."

"Were you afraid?"

"The whole time," said Anansi. "Every single step."

Elephant nodded her great head. "That's how it works," she said. "Brave isn't the same as not scared. Brave is scared and walking anyway."

That night, Anansi played the drum at the edge of the forest. Turtle came out of her shell to listen. Parrot sat close, even though her colors didn't show in the moonlight. Elephant swayed back and forth, and the ground swayed with her.

And the stories came — one after another after another — like rain on a wide green leaf.

---

*Snuggle close. Close your eyes. You were brave today too — even if nobody saw it. Even if the only one who knows is you.*

### Read-Aloud Tips

- **The refrain** ("Small legs, strong heart, one step more in the dark") — invite your child to say it with you by the second time. Whisper it. Let it be a spell.
- **The knocking knees** — tap your fingers on the book or on your child's back: *tak-tak-tak-tak-tak-tak-tak-tak*. They'll laugh. That's good. Laughing at fear is part of the medicine.
- **The drum sounds** — slow down for the *boom, boom, boom*. Let each one land. Your child's breathing will slow with the rhythm.
- **"Were you afraid?" / "The whole time."** — This is the most important line in the story. Don't rush it. Let the pause after "Every single step" sit for a beat.

### Three-Filter Analysis

| Filter | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Useful** | Builds courage — models that bravery is not the absence of fear but action despite fear. Also models asking for help (Anansi asks three friends) and the value of a waiting, steady presence (Elephant). |
| **Fits the data** | Aligns with exposure therapy research — graduated approach to feared stimulus within a safe relationship. The refrain gives the child agency (they control the "dose" through repetition). Developmentally appropriate for ages 3–7. |
| **Compassionate** | Anansi is never shamed for his fear. Elephant doesn't push him in — she promises to wait, and to come get him if needed. The darkness is real but survivable. The container (Elephant, the refrain, the reader's body) holds throughout. |

### Source Tradition Note

This story is inspired by the **Anansi (Anansesem) tradition** of the Akan and Ashanti peoples of West Africa, carried through the Atlantic diaspora to the Caribbean, where Anansi became the central figure in a rich body of resistance and survival stories. In the original tradition, Anansi acquires all the world's stories from Nyame, the Sky God, through cleverness rather than strength. The spider-as-storyteller motif — the smallest creature claiming the most powerful technology — is a founding myth of narrative itself. This is not a retelling of any specific traditional tale, but a new story written in the spirit of Anansi's courage and cunning. For the original acquisition myth, see Captain R.S. Rattray, *Akan-Ashanti Folk-Tales* (1930).
