# The Girl Who Planted in the Ruins

*Book 2, Story B — When Someone Needs Help*

---

Before there was ground, there was water. Before there was water, there was sky. And in the sky, there was a woman with seeds in her hands.

Her name was Skywoman, and she was falling.

Not the bad kind of falling — not the tripping, stumbling, uh-oh kind. The long kind. The slow kind. The kind of falling that gives you time to think about what you're going to do when you land.

Skywoman held her seeds tight. She had grabbed them on the way down — corn and beans and squash and one small handful of sweetgrass — because even when everything is breaking apart, some people think about what to plant.

---

The birds saw her first.

"Someone is falling!" said Goose.

"Someone needs help!" said Crane.

They flew up — all of them, every bird who had wings and a willing heart — and they caught her. Not one bird could have held her alone. But together, wing against wing against wing, they made a net of feathers and breath and trying.

*"Alone, too heavy. Together, we hold."*

They carried her down, slowly, gently, to the great water below. But there was no land. Just ocean, stretching in every direction, wide and dark and deep.

"We need earth," said Skywoman. "Just a handful. Just enough to start."

---

Loon dove first. Loon was brave and quick. She plunged deep, deeper, down into the dark where the water turned cold and the light forgot how to reach. She came back gasping, empty-clawed. "Too deep," she said. "I couldn't reach the bottom."

Otter dove next. Otter was strong and sleek. He twisted through the current, down and down, until his lungs burned and his eyes blurred. He came back shaking. "Too far," he said. "I couldn't hold my breath long enough."

*"Alone, too heavy. Together, we hold."*

Then Muskrat stepped forward. Muskrat was small. Smaller than Loon. Smaller than Otter. Nobody thought Muskrat could do what they couldn't.

Muskrat dove.

Down. Down. Down past where the light stopped. Down past where the cold started. Down to the very bottom of the world, where the mud was thick and dark and waiting.

She grabbed a tiny fistful. Just a little. Just enough.

When they pulled her back up, she was barely breathing. But her paw was closed tight, and inside it — mud. A smear of earth no bigger than a acorn.

---

They put the mud on Turtle's back. Turtle was patient. Turtle was steady. Turtle said, "I will hold this. For as long as it takes. I will hold this."

Skywoman stepped onto the mud. It was so small she could barely stand on it. But she didn't complain. She didn't wait for more. She started to dance.

Not a big dance. A quiet one. A grateful one. Her feet moved in a circle, and with each step, the earth beneath her grew. A little wider. A little firmer. A little more like home.

And when the ground was big enough — just barely big enough — she knelt down and planted her seeds.

*"Alone, too heavy. Together, we hold."*

The seeds grew. The corn came first, tall and green. Then the beans, climbing up the corn like it had been waiting for something to hold onto. Then the squash, spreading wide leaves across the ground like a blanket. The Three Sisters — corn, beans, and squash — growing together the way they were always meant to: each one helping the others, none of them alone.

---

That is how the world began. Not with a big bang. Not with a fight. With falling, and catching, and diving, and planting, and growing.

With someone saying: "I will hold this."
With someone else saying: "Let me try."
With the smallest one reaching the deepest mud.
With seeds in a fist and a dance on a turtle's back.

---

*Snuggle close. Close your eyes. The world is still growing. And tomorrow, you might be the one who dives deep, or the one who holds steady, or the one who plants something new. Every part matters. Even the small ones. Especially the small ones.*

---

### Read-Aloud Tips

- **The falling** — make it slow and dreamy, not scary. Skywoman is falling with purpose.
- **The refrain** ("Alone, too heavy. Together, we hold.") — this one works beautifully as a whisper. Let your child whisper it with you.
- **Muskrat's dive** — slow down on each "Down." Let the silence build. When Muskrat comes back with the mud, your voice should lift — relief, wonder, pride.
- **The Three Sisters** — if your child asks, explain: corn gives beans something to climb, beans put food back in the soil for the corn, squash covers the ground to keep it wet for everyone. They literally help each other grow. Like a family.

| Filter | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Useful** | Builds cooperation and persistence — models that big problems require collective effort, that the smallest contributor can make the biggest difference, and that creation starts with planting, not conquering. |
| **Fits the data** | Aligns with prosocial development — children this age are learning cooperation and role-differentiation. The story shows different strengths (Loon's bravery, Otter's strength, Muskrat's persistence, Turtle's patience) all contributing to a shared goal. Ecologically accurate Three Sisters companion planting. |
| **Compassionate** | No one is shamed for failing. Loon and Otter tried and couldn't reach — and that's honored. Muskrat succeeds not because she's better, but because she doesn't give up. The creation of the world begins with gratitude (the dance), not dominion. |

### Source Tradition Note

This story is inspired by the **Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) creation narrative** — specifically the Skywoman story as shared by Haudenosaunee scholars and elders, and as discussed by Robin Wall Kimmerer (Citizen Potawatomi Nation) in *Braiding Sweetgrass*. The original narrative establishes humans as grateful participants in ecosystems, not rulers over them. Skywoman's first act is planting — creation through nurture, not conquest. The Three Sisters agricultural system (corn, beans, squash) is a real companion planting technique developed by Indigenous peoples of the Americas over thousands of years. This is not a retelling of any specific sacred narrative but a new story written in the spirit of Haudenosaunee gratitude and ecological reciprocity.
