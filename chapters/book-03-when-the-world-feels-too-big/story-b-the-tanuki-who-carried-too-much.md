# The Tanuki Who Carried Too Much

*Book 3, Story B — When the World Feels Too Big*

---

In a forest where the maples turned red before anything else, there lived a tanuki named Hana.

A tanuki is a raccoon-dog — round belly, striped face, fluffy tail, and eyes that always look like they're in on a very good joke. Tanuki are famous for two things: being cheerful, and being able to carry enormous loads on their backs.

Hana was very good at both. Too good, maybe, at the second one.

---

It started with acorns.

"Hana," said Squirrel, "could you carry these acorns to my tree? My paws are full."

"Of course!" said Hana. She put the acorns on her back.

Then Frog asked her to carry his lily pads to the pond. Then Crow asked her to carry shiny stones to her nest. Then Badger asked her to carry mushrooms to his burrow, and Deer asked her to carry moss to her fawn's bed, and Beetle asked her to carry — well, Beetle asked her to carry Beetle, because Beetle's legs were tired.

Hana said yes to everyone. She always said yes. Saying yes felt warm, like sunlight on her belly. Saying no felt cold, like stepping in a puddle you didn't see coming.

So she carried the acorns and the lily pads and the shiny stones and the mushrooms and the moss and Beetle, and her back began to hurt, and her legs began to shake, and the path began to blur.

*"I can carry it. I can carry it. I can carry it all."*

She said it like a song. But it was starting to sound like a lie.

---

She was halfway up the hill when her legs gave out.

Everything tumbled — acorns rolling, lily pads flying, shiny stones scattering, mushrooms bouncing, moss drifting, and Beetle sailing through the air shouting, "WHEEEEE!" because Beetle didn't understand the situation at all.

Hana sat in the middle of the mess. Her back ached. Her eyes stung. The hill looked enormous above her and the forest looked enormous below her and the sky looked enormous everywhere and she felt very, very small.

"I can't carry it all," she whispered. And the whisper felt like the truest thing she'd said all day.

---

Old Turtle found her there.

Old Turtle was slow and patient and had been around long enough to have seen this kind of thing before.

"Hana," said Old Turtle. "What are you doing?"

"I'm carrying everyone's things to where they need to go."

"Why?"

Hana blinked. Nobody had ever asked her why. "Because they asked me to."

Old Turtle settled down next to her. His shell made a comfortable clicking sound against the rocks. "Can I tell you a secret?"

Hana nodded.

"Carrying things for people is kind. But carrying everything for everyone is not kind. It's just heavy."

*"Not every load is mine to carry. Not every 'yes' is mine to give."*

Hana's eyes went wide. "But if I say no, they'll be upset."

"Maybe," said Old Turtle. "For a little while. But if you say yes until you break, everyone loses their things anyway. Look around."

Hana looked. Acorns in the bushes. Lily pads in the dirt. Shiny stones everywhere. Everything she'd tried to carry was scattered worse than if she'd never picked it up at all.

---

So Hana tried something new.

She carried Squirrel's acorns to the tree, because the tree was close and her back felt fine.

She said to Frog, "I can walk with you to the pond, but you'll need to carry your own lily pads." Frog blinked twice, then picked them up himself. His arms were stronger than he thought.

She said to Crow, "I can't today. My back needs rest." Crow tilted her head, then flew the stones home herself. It took two trips. She didn't mind.

She said to Beetle, "You can ride on my head, but only to the next tree." Beetle said, "Deal!" and they both laughed, because Beetle laughing is a very small sound and Hana laughing is a very big sound and together they sounded like music.

*"Not every load is mine to carry. Not every 'yes' is mine to give."*

---

That evening, Hana sat at the top of the hill. Her back didn't hurt. Her legs were steady. The sunset turned the maples even redder than usual, and the world — the big, enormous, too-much world — felt exactly the right size.

Not because it had gotten smaller.

Because she had stopped trying to carry all of it at once.

---

*Snuggle close. Close your eyes. Tomorrow, if someone asks you to carry something, you can say yes. Or you can say "not today." Or you can say "I'll walk with you, but you carry your own lily pads." All three are kind. All three are brave.*

---

### Read-Aloud Tips

- **The escalating asks** — speed up slightly with each new animal asking. Build the comedic overload. Beetle asking to be carried is the punchline — ham it up.
- **The collapse** — Beetle shouting "WHEEEEE!" should get a laugh. Let it. Laughter at the disaster releases the tension before the tender part.
- **The refrain** ("Not every load is mine to carry. Not every 'yes' is mine to give.") — say this one slowly and seriously. It's medicine for people-pleasers of all ages, including the one reading aloud.
- **Old Turtle** — give him the slowest, kindest, most unhurried voice you've got. He has all the time in the world.

### Three-Filter Analysis

| Filter | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Useful** | Builds boundary-setting — models that saying no is a form of kindness, not selfishness. Also models that over-helping can backfire (everything scattered worse than before). Teaches the difference between generosity and self-depletion. |
| **Fits the data** | Aligns with developmental research on people-pleasing and boundary formation in early childhood. Children aged 3-7 are learning to differentiate self from others' needs. The story validates that helping is good AND that limits are necessary — both/and, not either/or. |
| **Compassionate** | Hana is never shamed for saying yes too much. Old Turtle doesn't scold — he shares a secret. The animals who are told "no" adapt without drama. The story normalizes boundary-setting as an act of care, not rejection. |

### Source Tradition Note

This story is inspired by **Japanese mukashi-banashi** (folk tales) and the tanuki tradition in Japanese storytelling. Tanuki (raccoon-dogs) are beloved figures in Japanese folklore — shape-shifters, tricksters, and cheerful helpers who sometimes get in over their heads. The Japanese yomikikase (read-aloud) tradition emphasizes rhythm, repetition, and the warmth of shared reading. This is not a retelling of any specific Japanese folk tale but a new story written in the spirit of mukashi-banashi's gentle humor and the yomikikase tradition's emphasis on relational warmth.
