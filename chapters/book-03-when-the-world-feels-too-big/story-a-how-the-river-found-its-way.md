# How the River Found Its Way

*Book 3, Story A — When the World Feels Too Big*

---

A long, long time ago — so long ago that the rocks were still soft and the stars hadn't decided where to sit — a river woke up.

She didn't know she was a river yet. She was just water. A small puddle of water, high on a mountain, blinking at the sky.

"Where am I supposed to go?" she asked.

Nobody answered. The mountain was quiet. The sky was big. The world stretched out below her in every direction — forests and deserts and valleys and plains — and she didn't know which way was hers.

---

She asked the Eagle.

"Eagle," she said, "you can see everything from up there. Which way should I go?"

Eagle circled once, twice, three times. "I can see all the ways," Eagle said. "But I can't tell you which one is yours. That's not how it works."

She asked the Rock.

"Rock," she said, "you've been here since the beginning. Which way should I go?"

Rock was warm from the sun. "I don't go anywhere," Rock said. "That's my gift. Yours is different."

She asked the Wind.

"Wind," she said, "you go everywhere. Which way should I go?"

Wind laughed — not a mean laugh, a gentle one. "I go everywhere because I don't have a bed. You will make yours as you go."

---

So the river started moving. She didn't know where she was going. She just went down, because down was easy, because water knows one thing for certain: low places need filling.

She trickled at first. A tiny thread of water, barely wider than a lizard's tongue. She slipped over pebbles and under roots and between the toes of sleeping frogs.

*"I don't know the way, but I know how to move."*

She came to a boulder. Enormous. Solid. Right in her path.

She couldn't go through it. She couldn't go over it. So she went around it — slowly, patiently, curving left where she'd been going straight, finding a gap the boulder didn't even know it had.

And something surprising happened: the curve was beautiful. The bend made a pool, and in the pool, fish came to live. Dragonflies visited. A family of ducks decided this was exactly the spot they'd been looking for.

The obstacle made the river more interesting. More alive. More herself.

*"I don't know the way, but I know how to move."*

---

She came to a cliff. The ground dropped away beneath her, sudden and steep, and there was nothing to do but fall.

So she fell.

She fell a long way — tumbling, roaring, splitting into a thousand bright pieces against the rocks below. It was loud. It was wild. It was the most frightening thing that had ever happened to her.

But at the bottom, she gathered herself back together. She was wider now. Stronger. The falling had broken her into pieces, and the pieces had come back together into something bigger than before.

She was a waterfall, and she hadn't even known that was a thing she could be.

*"I don't know the way, but I know how to move."*

---

Days passed. Weeks. Seasons.

The river grew. Other streams joined her — small trickles from other mountains, each one saying, "Can I come too?" and the river always said yes. She got wider. Deeper. She carved her own valley. Trees grew along her edges, and their roots held her banks, and she held their roots, and they needed each other in the way that neighbors do.

And then one day — after the mountains and the boulders and the cliff and the valley and the long, winding, not-knowing journey — she smelled something different in the air. Salt. Space. A bigness even bigger than the sky on the mountain.

The ocean.

She hadn't known she was heading there. She hadn't had a map. She'd just kept moving — around the boulders, over the cliffs, through the valleys — and the moving itself had been the way.

She poured into the ocean, and the ocean said, "There you are. I've been waiting."

---

*Snuggle close. Close your eyes. You don't have to know where you're going yet. You just have to keep moving. Around the hard parts. Through the scary parts. The path is the one you make by walking it.*

### Read-Aloud Tips

- **The three advisors** — give Eagle a high, distant voice. Rock a slow, warm rumble. Wind a playful, breezy whisper.
- **The refrain** ("I don't know the way, but I know how to move.") — this one is great with a gentle rocking motion. Rock your child slightly as you say it.
- **The waterfall** — speed up your voice for the fall. Then slow way down for "But at the bottom, she gathered herself back together." Let the calm after the chaos do the work.
- **"There you are. I've been waiting."** — say this directly to your child. Look at them. Mean it.

### Three-Filter Analysis

| Filter | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Useful** | Builds tolerance for uncertainty — models that not knowing the destination is okay when you trust the process. Also models resilience: the boulder makes the river more beautiful, the cliff makes her stronger. Obstacles are incorporated, not merely overcome. |
| **Fits the data** | Aligns with developmental research on frustration tolerance and uncertainty management — key tasks for ages 3-7. The three advisors model different ways of being (stillness, vision, movement) without any being wrong. Aboriginal Dreamtime creation narratives frequently describe landscape formation through the actions of ancestor beings. |
| **Compassionate** | The river is never told she should already know where to go. Every advisor gives honest, respectful answers. The ocean's "I've been waiting" tells the child that belonging exists even before you find it. |

### Source Tradition Note

This story is inspired by **Aboriginal Australian Dreamtime narratives** in which ancestor beings create the landscape through their actions — rivers carved by Rainbow Serpent, mountains raised by ancestral footsteps, waterholes created by digging sticks. In these traditions, the land is not a passive backdrop but a living record of creation, and every landscape feature encodes ecological and spiritual knowledge. Aboriginal songlines can extend over 3,500 kilometers, encoding navigation, ecology, law, and ceremony simultaneously. This is not a retelling of any specific Dreamtime story (many of which are sacred and restricted) but a new story written in the spirit of landscape as living narrative and journey as self-discovery.
