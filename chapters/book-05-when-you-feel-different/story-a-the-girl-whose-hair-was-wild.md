# The Girl Whose Hair Was Wild

*Book 5, Story A — When You Feel Different*

---

Maren's hair did not behave.

Other children's hair lay flat and smooth and did what it was told. Maren's hair went up, and out, and sideways, and sometimes — when it rained — in directions that didn't have names yet.

Her mother braided it. It unbraided itself by lunch. Her father combed it. The comb got lost somewhere near her left ear and was never seen again. Her grandmother tried a ribbon. The ribbon surrendered.

"Why can't my hair be normal?" Maren asked.

Nobody had a very good answer.

---

At school, the other children stared. Not the good kind of staring — the kind where your eyes get wide because something is beautiful. The other kind. The pointing kind. The whispering kind.

"Your hair looks like a bird's nest," said a boy named Lars.

"Your hair looks like a thundercloud," said a girl named Petra.

Maren's cheeks burned. She pulled her hood up and kept it up for the rest of the day. Under the hood, her hair pressed against her head, flat and cramped and still.

It felt wrong. The way shoes feel wrong on the wrong feet. The way holding your breath feels wrong after the first five seconds.

But at least nobody stared.

---

That night, Maren couldn't sleep. Her hood was off, and her hair was doing what it always did — spreading across the pillow like a dark forest, curling around her fingers, reaching for the moonlight through the window.

She heard a sound. A tiny sound, like seeds falling on leaves.

She looked up. A bird was sitting on her windowsill. Not a regular bird — a bird made of silver and shadow, with eyes like two small moons.

"Why are you hiding your hair?" said the bird.

"Because it's different," said Maren.

"Different from what?"

Maren thought. "From everyone else's."

The bird tilted its head. "Come with me. I want to show you something."

*"What grows from you is part of you. What's part of you is not a mistake."*

---

The bird flew, and Maren followed — out the window, over the rooftops, into the forest at the edge of town. The forest she'd been told not to enter after dark.

But the bird was made of moonlight, and moonlight doesn't go to dangerous places. It goes to true places.

They came to a clearing. In the clearing stood the oldest tree Maren had ever seen. Its branches twisted in every direction — not straight, not neat, not orderly. Wild. Reaching. Alive in a way that made the word "messy" feel like a compliment.

"This tree is the oldest living thing in this forest," said the bird. "Three hundred years. Its roots hold the riverbank. Its branches shelter forty-seven species of bird. Its acorns feed the deer who feed the wolves who keep the deer from eating all the young trees."

Maren stared at the wild, wonderful branches.

"Does anyone tell this tree to be straight?" asked the bird.

*"What grows from you is part of you. What's part of you is not a mistake."*

---

They flew further. Over a meadow where wildflowers grew in tangled, chaotic profusion — no rows, no order, just color exploding in every direction. A river that curved and doubled back on itself, making pools where otters played and fish hid and dragonflies danced on the still water.

"The river could be straight," said the bird. "People have tried. They put it in concrete channels and pointed it where they wanted it to go. Do you know what happened?"

"What?"

"The fish died. The otters left. The dragonflies disappeared. When the floods came, the straight river couldn't hold the water because there were no curves to slow it down. The bends weren't flaws. They were the river doing its work."

Maren touched her hair. It curled around her fingers, the way it always did, reaching for something she couldn't see.

*"What grows from you is part of you. What's part of you is not a mistake."*

---

When she woke up the next morning, Maren's hair was wilder than ever. It had opinions about Tuesday. It had plans for the afternoon. It went in fourteen directions, and every single one of them was right.

She did not pull up her hood.

She walked to school with her hair doing exactly what it wanted to do. Lars stared. Petra whispered. Maren kept walking.

And a funny thing happened. Not right away — not that day, maybe not even that week. But slowly, the way rivers carve stone and trees grow rings, the staring changed. First to curiosity. Then to something that looked almost like admiration. And then — one Tuesday in October — a girl named Signe came up to Maren and said, very quietly, "I wish my hair did that."

Maren smiled. "It does something else," she said. "Something only yours can do. You just have to stop making it lie flat."

---

*Snuggle close. Close your eyes. Whatever is wild about you — the thing that won't lie flat, the thing that doesn't match, the thing that goes in directions nobody expects — that's not the part of you that's broken. That's the part of you that's most alive.*

---

### Read-Aloud Tips

- **The hair** — make it a character. Give it personality. When it "has opinions about Tuesday," say it like you're reporting the news about a very opinionated friend.
- **Lars and Petra** — don't make them villains. Make them boring. Flat voices, unimaginative insults. The contrast with Maren's wild aliveness is the point.
- **The bird** — whisper-voiced, silvery, kind. Not magical-sparkly. Magical-true.
- **The refrain** ("What grows from you is part of you. What's part of you is not a mistake.") — say this one to your child, not to the room. Eye contact. Mean it. This might be the most important refrain in the whole collection.
- **The tree and the river** — these are real ecological principles. If your child asks, the science is true: old-growth trees really do hold ecosystems together, and straightened rivers really do lose their biodiversity.

### Three-Filter Analysis

| Filter | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Useful** | Builds self-acceptance — models that the thing that makes you different might be the thing that makes you essential. Also builds ecological thinking: the "messy" tree and the "crooked" river serve functions that their "neat" versions cannot. |
| **Fits the data** | Aligns with self-concept development in ages 3-7 — children are forming core beliefs about whether their differences are deficits or gifts. The ecological metaphors are scientifically accurate. Research on old-growth forests and river channelization confirms every claim the bird makes. |
| **Compassionate** | Maren is never told to "just ignore them." Her hurt is real. The bird doesn't fix the other children — it changes Maren's relationship to herself. The resolution is not that everyone accepts her (that comes slowly, realistically) but that she accepts herself first. |

### Source Tradition Note

This story is inspired by the **European folk tale tradition** — specifically the pre-Grimm oral tradition in which female protagonists were active, clever, and self-determining before the Grimms' editorial project systematically reduced female agency across seven editions (1812-1857). As Ruth Bottigheimer documented in *Grimms' Bad Girls and Bold Boys* (1987), speech was "systematically taken away from women and given to men" through successive editions. In the original oral *veillée* tradition, stories were told by women in communal spaces, and female characters were far wilder than their sanitized published versions. This story is written in the spirit of those wilder originals — a girl whose difference is her power, not her flaw.
