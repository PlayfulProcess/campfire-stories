# The Boy Who Thanked Everything

*Book 5, Story B — When You Feel Different*

---

Koda thanked things.

Not just people. Things.

"Thank you, door," he said every morning when it opened.

"Thank you, shoes," he said when they carried him to school.

"Thank you, rain," he said when it fell on his face, even when it was cold, even when it made his socks wet and his teacher frown.

The other children thought this was strange. "Doors can't hear you," said Marcus. "Shoes don't care," said June. "Rain definitely doesn't know your name," said a boy named Theo, who was certain about most things.

Koda kept thanking anyway. Not because he thought doors could hear. Because thanking felt like breathing — something his body wanted to do, something that made the world fit better, something that turned ordinary things into gifts.

---

His grandmother understood.

She was Haudenosaunee — Mohawk, from the Wolf Clan — and every morning before breakfast she stood on the back porch and said words that Koda had been hearing since before he could walk.

"We give thanks to the Earth, our Mother," she began. Then the waters. Then the fish. Then the plants, the food plants, the medicine herbs, the animals, the trees, the birds. The four winds. The thunderers. The sun, the moon, the stars. The teachers. The Creator.

Every element of the living world, named and thanked, in order, before a single bite of toast.

*"We give thanks, and now our minds are one."*

"Grandma," Koda asked once, "do you think the trees hear you?"

She smiled. "I think I hear myself," she said. "And that changes how I walk through the day. When you thank the door, you notice the door. When you notice the door, you take care of the door. When you take care of things, things take care of you back. It's a circle."

---

At school, Koda's class was assigned a project: take care of something for one month.

Marcus chose a cactus. "It barely needs water," he said. "Easy."

June chose a goldfish. "It's just one fish," she said. "How hard can it be?"

Theo chose nothing. "This project is pointless," he said. Theo was certain about that too.

Koda looked around the classroom. In the corner, there was a garden box. It had been there since September — filled with dirt, forgotten, growing nothing but dust and one very confused ant.

"I'll take the garden," he said.

---

Every morning, Koda came in early. He turned the soil. He planted seeds — lettuce, radishes, sunflowers. He watered them. And he thanked them.

"Thank you, soil, for holding the seeds."

"Thank you, water, for waking them up."

"Thank you, sunlight, for reaching through the window."

Marcus's cactus turned brown. He'd overwatered it. "I thought barely any meant none," he said sadly.

June's goldfish got sick. She'd forgotten to change the water. "I didn't know water could go bad," she said.

Theo watched Koda talk to the dirt every morning. "You know that's weird, right?"

"Probably," said Koda.

*"We give thanks, and now our minds are one."*

---

The radishes came first — small red planets pushing up through the dark soil. Then the lettuce, bright green and rumpled like tiny bedsheets. Then the sunflowers, climbing higher and higher, until they reached the window and turned their faces toward the light like children looking up at something wonderful.

The class gathered around.

"How did you do that?" asked Marcus.

"I paid attention," said Koda. "When you thank something, you have to look at it. When you look at it, you see what it needs. The soil was dry on Wednesdays — the heater blows harder then. The lettuce on the left gets less light, so I rotated it. The sunflowers needed stakes at week three or they'd fall over."

"You learned all that from talking to dirt?"

Koda thought about it. "I learned it from listening. The thanking is what makes me listen."

---

Theo came up to Koda after class. Quietly. Looking at the floor.

"Can I help with the garden?" he asked. "I didn't pick anything, and now I wish I had."

"Sure," said Koda. "Start with the radishes. They're easy. They just need water and noticing."

Theo knelt down next to the garden box. He looked at the radishes — really looked, maybe for the first time.

"Thank you, radishes," he said. Very quietly. Like he was trying on a coat he wasn't sure fit yet.

It fit.

*"We give thanks, and now our minds are one."*

---

That evening, Koda stood on the back porch with his grandmother. The sky was doing its sunset thing — oranges and pinks and one long streak of purple that looked like a river in the sky.

"Grandma," he said. "The kids at school think I'm weird."

"You are weird," she said. "Weird means your gift hasn't found its people yet. It will."

"How do you know?"

She put her arm around him. "Because the garden grew. And that boy asked to help. And now he's thanking radishes." She laughed. "When one person thanks out loud, it gives everyone else permission to thank quietly. That's how it spreads."

*"We give thanks, and now our minds are one."*

Koda leaned into her. The sky turned deeper. The stars were coming, one by one, like guests arriving at a party they'd been invited to since the beginning of time.

"Thank you, stars," he said.

"Thank you, stars," she said.

---

*Snuggle close. Close your eyes. Tomorrow, try thanking one thing. Just one. Your bed, for holding you. Your breakfast, for keeping you strong. The door, for letting you through. You might feel silly. That's okay. Thank it anyway. And see if the world fits a little better after.*

### Read-Aloud Tips

- **Koda's thanking** — matter-of-fact, not performative. He's not showing off. He's just doing what feels right.
- **Grandma's address** — if you feel comfortable, actually list a few elements: "the earth, the waters, the fish, the plants..." Let the rhythm build. This is real — the Ohèn:ton Karihwatéhkwen (Thanksgiving Address) opens every Haudenosaunee gathering.
- **The refrain** ("We give thanks, and now our minds are one.") — this is the actual closing refrain of the Thanksgiving Address. Say it with reverence. It's not a fictional invention.
- **Theo's "Thank you, radishes"** — this is the emotional peak. Say it the way a child would say something brave and uncertain and true. Quiet. Almost a whisper. Then: "It fit." Let that land.
- **"Weird means your gift hasn't found its people yet"** — pause after this. It might be medicine for the adult reading aloud, too.

### Source Tradition Note

This story is inspired by the **Ohèn:ton Karihwatéhkwen** (Thanksgiving Address, or "Words Before All Else") of the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy. This communal recitation systematically acknowledges every element of the living world and closes each section with the refrain "Now our minds are one." It opens every social and religious gathering, every council meeting, and every school day at the Akwesasne Freedom School and the Onondaga Nation School. Robin Wall Kimmerer (Citizen Potawatomi Nation) describes it as "simultaneously a political structure, a Bill of Responsibilities, an educational model, a family tree, and a scientific inventory of ecosystem services." The Thanksgiving Address is not fiction — it is a living practice. This story honors it by showing how its principles work in a child's daily life, without claiming to reproduce or represent the Address itself.
