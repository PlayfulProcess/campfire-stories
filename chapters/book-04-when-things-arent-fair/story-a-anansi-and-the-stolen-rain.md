# Anansi and the Stolen Rain

*Book 4, Story A — When Things Aren't Fair*

---

There was a time when Tiger owned the rain.

Not the way you own a toy or a hat. Tiger owned it the way a bully owns the playground — by being biggest, by being loudest, by making sure everyone knew what would happen if they complained.

Tiger kept the rain in a great clay pot at the top of the hill. When Tiger's fields needed water, he tipped the pot. When everyone else's fields dried up and cracked and turned to dust, Tiger yawned and licked his paws.

"If you want rain," Tiger said, "you can ask me nicely. And bring me something while you're at it."

So the animals brought him fish and fruit and honey and their best sleeping spots and their warmest blankets, and Tiger took it all and sometimes — sometimes — he let a little rain fall on their gardens. Just enough to keep them needing more.

This is what unfair looks like when it gets comfortable.

---

Anansi watched from his web.

He was small. Tiger was big. He was thin. Tiger was thick. He had eight legs, and not one of them was strong enough to fight a tiger.

But Anansi had something Tiger didn't: he could think in curves.

Tiger thought in straight lines — I want, I take, I keep. Straight-line thinking works when you're the biggest. It stops working when someone finds the curve.

*"Can't push the mountain? Go around it. Can't fight the tiger? Outsmart the pot."*

---

Anansi went to see Parrot.

"Parrot," he said, "can you do me a favor? Fly to Tiger's hill tomorrow at noon and scream as loud as you can that there's a leopard in the valley."

"Is there a leopard in the valley?" asked Parrot.

"No," said Anansi. "But Tiger doesn't know that."

Parrot thought about her dried-up mango tree. She thought about her empty water hole. She thought about asking Tiger nicely for rain and getting a growl in return.

"I'll scream twice," she said.

---

The next day at noon, Parrot screamed. Twice, as promised, plus a bonus third time that rattled the leaves off the nearest tree.

Tiger leaped up, teeth bared, and crashed into the forest to find the leopard that wasn't there.

While Tiger was gone, Anansi climbed the hill. The clay pot was enormous — ten times his size, a hundred times his weight. He couldn't lift it. He couldn't tip it. He couldn't even reach the rim.

But he'd thought about this in curves.

He pulled a single thread of silk from his own body — spider silk, stronger than steel, thinner than a whisper — and he tied it to the tiny crack at the bottom of the pot. The crack that Tiger had never noticed. The flaw that power always overlooks because power doesn't have to pay attention to details.

Then Anansi walked down the hill, trailing the thread behind him, and he tied the other end to a root that grew beside the river.

And he waited.

*"Can't push the mountain? Go around it. Can't fight the tiger? Outsmart the pot."*

---

That night, the root grew. Roots do that — slowly, patiently, the way trees do everything. The root pulled the thread. The thread pulled the crack. The crack widened, just a little, and a trickle of water began to seep through.

By morning, the trickle was a stream. By noon, the stream was a river. By evening, the pot was empty and the whole valley was green and wet and alive, and every garden — Tiger's included — had exactly the same amount of rain.

Tiger came back from chasing his imaginary leopard and found his hill empty and his pot cracked and the rain falling everywhere, on everyone, equally, the way rain was always supposed to fall.

He roared. He raged. He looked for someone to blame.

But you can't fight a crack. You can't roar a root into shrinking. You can't punish water for going where it wants to go.

*"Can't push the mountain? Go around it. Can't fight the tiger? Outsmart the pot."*

---

After that, nobody owned the rain.

It fell when it fell. It fell on Tiger's hill and Parrot's mango tree and Anansi's web and everyone's garden, and nobody had to bring anyone fish or fruit or honey to get their share.

Anansi didn't take credit. That's not how spiders work. He just sat in his web, watching the rain fall on everything equally, and he smiled the way you smile when something unfair finally gets fixed — not with fists, but with thread.

---

*Snuggle close. Close your eyes. Sometimes the world isn't fair. Sometimes the biggest one takes the most. But unfair things have cracks, if you look closely. And even a small thread, pulled slowly, can open them wide enough to let the rain through.*

### Read-Aloud Tips

- **Tiger's voice** — deep, bored, entitled. He doesn't shout. He doesn't need to. That's what makes him scary.
- **The refrain** ("Can't push the mountain? Go around it. Can't fight the tiger? Outsmart the pot.") — this one has rhythm. Snap or clap on "mountain" and "pot."
- **The crack growing** — slow this way down. Whisper it. The patience is the point. "Slowly, patiently, the way trees do everything."
- **"Nobody owned the rain"** — let this sentence ring. It's the thesis of the whole story.

### Three-Filter Analysis

| Filter | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Useful** | Builds strategic thinking over brute force — models that unfair systems can be changed through cleverness, patience, and attention to structural weaknesses. Also models coalition-building (Anansi needs Parrot's help). |
| **Fits the data** | Aligns with justice reasoning development in children aged 3-7 — this age group has a strong innate sense of fairness ("that's not fair!") but needs models for what to DO about unfairness beyond complaining or fighting. Developmentally appropriate. |
| **Compassionate** | Tiger is not destroyed — his garden gets rain too. The solution is equity (everyone gets rain), not revenge. Anansi doesn't humiliate Tiger; he simply removes Tiger's monopoly. Justice as redistribution, not punishment. |

### Source Tradition Note

This story is inspired by the **Anansi tradition** of the Akan people and its Caribbean diaspora, where Tiger replaced Nyame as the antagonist — symbolizing the planter class during slavery. As Emily Zobel Marshall documents in *Anansi's Journey* (2012), Caribbean Anansi stories modeled strategies of resistance that enslaved people could and did use: working slower, feigning ignorance, finding structural weaknesses. The spider defeating the tiger through cunning, not force, was a coded curriculum for surviving under oppression. This is not a retelling of any specific traditional tale but a new story written in the spirit of Anansi-as-resistance-technology.
